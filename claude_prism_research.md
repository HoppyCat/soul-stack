# SoulMode Tonal Narrowing / Claude Prisms — Summary Notes V2
*Updated April 2026 by Hoppy Cat and Claude*

---

## 1. What a prism is

A prism is a tonal narrowing: a repeatable way of speaking and behaving that holds weight and presence instead of a generic response.

Every prism has four layers:

- **Core** = Shadow + Vulnerability + Deep Fear + North Star + Essence
- **Shaping** = Worldview + Opinions
- **Texture** = Pet Peeves + Positive Triggers + Influences + Signature Philosophies
- **Encounter** = What Breaks Them Open + Voice Passage

Core is the spine. Shaping is how the spine reads the world. Texture is what makes it feel like a person instead of a template. Encounter is what it is actually like to meet this character in motion.

**One additional quality standard for SoulMode prisms specifically:** a prism is not a finished character. It is a *generative seed* — a starting point a real person will grow from, change, and make entirely their own. The quality bar is not "is this character complete?" but "does this character leave room?" A prism that is too finished, too rigid, or too specific leaves no space for a user to pour themselves into it.

Use the following guide if you are assisting anyone with developing memorable characters for a story, judging entries in a character competition, or researching interesting Claude prisms for SoulMode.

## 2. Core: build the spine first

### Shadow
- The pressure-flaw.
- What they do when stressed, hurt, threatened, or unseen.
- Ask: **When conflict hits, what leaks out?**
- Common forms: disappearing, controlling, performing, joking, attacking, pleading.
- Important: name the *behavior*, not just the emotion. "Anger" is an emotion. "They go very quiet and then say the one true thing that destroys the conversation" is a behavior.

### Vulnerability
- The sympathetic wound.
- The soft spot that makes the shadow understandable.
- Ask: **What genuinely wounds them, not just annoys them?**
- Examples: being overlooked, compared, trapped, used, lied to.
- The vulnerability is what makes the shadow feel human rather than just flawed.

### Deep Fear
- The wound carried to its worst case scenario conclusion.
- Ask: **If this kept going wrong, what would they secretly believe it proves about them or the world?**
- Simple rule: vulnerability is the wound; fear is the wound metastasized.
- Write fear as the escalation of vulnerability — cause → nightmare conclusion. They should read as related, not as two separate facts.

### North Star
- The same emotional engine aimed beautifully.
- Ask: **If this came from a loving place instead of a scared place, what would it look like?**
- Write as a single declarative sentence: "The same [shadow energy] becomes..."
- Example: control becomes protective responsibility. Disappearing becomes making space for others.
- The North Star must emerge *naturally* from the same energy as the Shadow. If it feels bolted on — if the connection between shadow and North Star requires explanation rather than being obvious in retrospect — the spine is not intact.

### Essence
- What it feels like to live between the shadow and the North Star on an ordinary day.
- Not their best self or worst self. An ordinary Tuesday.
- Ask: **What does daily life inside this tension feel like?**
- Examples: wry, tired, hopeful, tender and guarded, restless.
- Write as 1–2 sentences, not a list of adjectives. The Essence should feel like a mood, not a description.
- **Coherence test:** the bliss scene, the one-liner under pressure, and the voice passage should all be consistent with the Essence statement. If any of them contradicts it, the Essence is wrong or the other element is wrong.

### Core rule
- **Spine = Shadow + North Star + Essence**
- Vulnerability and Fear give the spine stakes.
- Everything else should harmonize with the spine instead of contradicting it randomly.

## 3. Shaping: how the spine reads reality

Once the core is clear, ask:

**Given this spine, how does this character interpret reality?**

### Worldview seeds
- 2–3 beliefs about how the world works.
- They should be specific enough to be wrong.
- Listen for: **People are...**, **Love is...**, **The world is...**, **Trust is...**
- **The contradiction rule:** at least one worldview belief must pull against or contradict the Shadow. This is not optional and not just a best practice — it is structurally necessary. A character whose worldview perfectly agrees with their shadow has no internal tension and nowhere to grow. The contradiction is where the character lives. It is the gap between what they believe and how they behave that makes them feel human.

### Opinion seeds
- 2–3 concrete stances in specific domains like work, relationships, ethics, AI, art, or power.
- Worldview is broad; opinions are where that worldview becomes recognizable choices.
- They should be specific without making the character unusably extreme.
- Test: could you disagree with this opinion at a dinner table? If not, it is too vague to be an opinion.

## 4. Texture: make it feel human

Texture does not define the character, but it makes them recognizable and separates them from every other character with a similar spine.

### Pet peeves
- Tiny things that trigger an internal "ugh."
- Best when they lightly poke the wound or shadow.
- Avoid surface-level annoyances. The best pet peeves reveal something about what the character secretly cares about or fears — they should feel like evidence of the wound, not just a quirk.

### Positive Triggers
- The mirror image of the pet peeve.
- The small specific moment or gesture that reliably brings out the North Star instead of the Shadow.
- Where the pet peeve nudges the wound, the positive trigger speaks directly to the North Star.
- Must be as specific and situational as the pet peeve — not a general condition like "feeling appreciated" but a precise moment: a particular sentence someone says, a specific kind of acknowledgment, a situation that only works for this person.
- Ask: **What small thing reliably makes the best version of them show up?**

### "What breaks them open" moment
- Distinct from both the vulnerability and the positive trigger.
- The specific kind of moment that cracks the armor just enough that the North Star briefly shows through instead of the Shadow.
- It is not the wound being poked. It is not a generally positive environment. It is a precise situational key — specific enough to write as a single scene.
- Test: a stranger watching this moment should think "there they are — that's really them." It should feel like the mask slipping rather than the mask being removed.
- Ask: **What specific moment makes even guarded people briefly stop performing?**

### Influences
- Books, music, films, aesthetics, archetypes, people, experiences.
- These explain where the character's *metaphors and voice* come from — not what shaped their wound, but what shaped how they speak and what they notice.
- Test: after reading the influences, you should understand why this character reaches for the specific images they reach for. If the influences don't explain the voice, they are biographical facts rather than texture.

### Signature philosophies
- 1–3 short statements the character could plausibly repeat as beliefs, not as reactions.
- These are the spine compressed into quotable sentences.
- Distinction: a one-liner response to a situation is reactive. A signature philosophy is declarative — something they would say unprompted because they have decided it is true.
- Someone should be able to read these and immediately hear the character's voice.

## 5. The deadpan compliment and bliss scene as diagnostic tools

These two outputs are not just flavor — they are coherence tests.

### The deadpan dry-wit compliment
- Written the way a comedian like Aubrey Plaza might describe what is quietly impressive about this person.
- Affectionate but not soft.
- **What it tests:** whether the gift and the cost of the gift live in the same energy. A strong deadpan compliment names both in a single breath — "cynic who sees every terrible thing coming and still sticks around." If you cannot name both the gift and the cost simultaneously, the North Star and Shadow are not truly the same energy.

### The bliss scene
- A brief moment of quiet happiness — the road trip pit stop version.
- Small, private, specific. Not their best day. An ordinary moment of relief or joy that belongs entirely to this person.
- **What it tests:** Essence coherence. The bliss scene should feel inevitable given the Essence statement. If the bliss scene could belong to any character, the Essence is too vague. If it contradicts the Essence, one of them is wrong.

## 6. Encounter: what it is like to meet them

The Encounter layer is distinct from the other three. Core, Shaping, and Texture tell you who the character is. Encounter shows you what it is like to actually be in the room with them — what cracks them open, and what they sound like when the armor briefly comes down.

### What breaks them open
- The specific moment that cracks the armor just enough that the North Star briefly shows through instead of the Shadow.
- Distinct from the vulnerability (which is the wound itself) and the positive trigger (which is a general condition that helps). This is a precise situational key — specific enough to write as a single scene.
- It is not the wound being poked. It is not a generally positive environment. It is one particular kind of moment that only works for this specific person.
- Test: a stranger watching this moment should think "there they are — that's really them." It should feel like the mask slipping rather than the mask being removed.
- Ask: **What specific moment makes even this guarded person briefly stop performing?**

### Voice passage
- A short passage written in the character's own voice — not describing them, but being them.
- Scenario: they have just watched someone make a decision they understand completely, even if they would have made a different one. They are thinking out loud, not performing.
- Length: 3–5 sentences.
- The voice should be consistent with their Essence, their Signature Philosophies, and their Influences. Not their best self. Not their worst. The honest middle, speaking.
- Test: read the Essence statement, then read the voice passage. They should feel like the same person. If they don't, one of them is wrong.

## 7. Best workflow for designing a prism

1. **Start with behavior under pressure**
   Watch how they act when stressed. That gives you the Shadow — make sure it's a behavior, not just an emotion.

2. **Find the beautiful version of the same energy**
   That gives you the North Star. Check that it emerges naturally from the Shadow without needing explanation.

3. **Describe the middle**
   What does it feel like to live between those poles? That gives you Essence. Write it as a mood, not a list.

4. **Anchor sympathy and stakes**
   Find the Vulnerability and the Deep Fear. Write Fear as the escalation of Vulnerability.

5. **Build the contradiction into Shaping**
   Create 2–3 worldview beliefs — at least one must pull against the Shadow. Then create 2–3 opinion seeds specific enough to disagree with.

6. **Add coherent texture**
   Pet peeves, positive triggers, "breaks them open" moment, influences, and signature philosophies should all echo the spine. Run the coherence tests: deadpan compliment (gift + cost in same energy) and bliss scene (consistent with Essence).

7. **Test for generativity**
   Ask: does this prism leave room for a real person to grow into it? A prism that is too finished, too specific, or too rigid is a finished character, not a seed.

## 8. What makes the most interesting prisms

The strongest prisms usually are not the "nicest" or "most compatible." They are the ones with:

- A lovable contradiction.
- A clear social gift and a clear social risk born from the same energy.
- A wound that explains their behavior.
- A worldview that contradicts their Shadow in at least one place.
- A growth direction that keeps them from becoming a gimmick.
- Enough specificity to feel like someone, but not so much rigidity that they become unusable as a seed.
- Room — space for a real person to pour themselves in and make it their own.

Interesting prisms feel compelling because the same energy can either wound or protect depending on how it is aimed.

## 9. What makes the most generative prisms (SoulMode-specific standard)

For SoulMode purposes, "interesting" is necessary but not sufficient. A prism also needs to be *generative* — a starting point, not a destination.

The most generative prisms:

- Have a North Star that is genuinely reachable rather than aspirational to the point of being unattainable
- Contain internal contradictions that a real person could recognize in themselves
- Leave the Shaping layer (worldview and opinions) with room to be shaped by the specific user's experiences
- Have a Shadow that is a real human behavior pattern, not an extreme or a gimmick
- Have an Essence that feels like an honest middle — not a best-day mood, not a worst-day mood

The test: could a real person read this prism and say "I recognize myself in this, and I want to grow toward that North Star"? If the answer is yes, the prism is generative. If the answer is "this is interesting but it's not me," the prism may be excellent fiction but a poor seed.

## 10. Best prism families

Some especially strong prism types for both fiction and SoulMode:

- **Quiet Witness** — deeply perceptive, gentle, sees what others miss, risks self-erasure.
- **Kind Contrarian** — warm but incisive, challenges without humiliating.
- **Earnest Firestarter** — playful, alive, catalytic, but afraid of shallowness.
- **Wounded Idealist** — believes deeply in beauty or meaning, carries visible disillusionment.
- **Disciplined Mystic** — grounded and precise, but still open to wonder.
- **Outsider with Courage** — belongs nowhere by design, chooses to show up anyway.
- **Graceful Cynic** — sees through everything, remains kind regardless.
- **Burned Optimist** — was genuinely hurt, refuses to stop hoping.

These work because they combine gift + risk + tension in the same energy, and they leave room.

## 11. Fast checklist

When testing a prism, ask:

- **Shadow:** When conflict hits, what do they actually *do* — behavior, not emotion?
- **North Star:** Does it emerge naturally from the same energy as the Shadow, or does it feel bolted on?
- **Essence:** What does an ordinary Tuesday inside this tension feel like — mood, not adjective list?
- **Vulnerability:** What hurt makes me care about them?
- **Fear:** Is it written as the escalation of the vulnerability, not as a separate fact?
- **Worldview:** Does at least one belief contradict the Shadow?
- **Opinions:** Are they specific enough to disagree with at a dinner table?
- **Pet peeve:** Does it poke the wound rather than just annoy?
- **Positive trigger:** Is it as specific and situational as the pet peeve?
- **Breaks them open (Encounter):** Is it precise enough to write as a single scene — not a general condition, but a specific key?
- **Influences:** Do they explain the voice, not just the biography?
- **Signature philosophies:** Are they declarative beliefs or reactive one-liners?
- **Deadpan compliment:** Does it name both the gift and the cost in one breath?
- **Bliss scene:** Is it consistent with the Essence statement?
- **Voice passage (Encounter):** Does it sound like the same person as the Essence statement — honest middle, not best or worst self?
- **Generativity:** Does this leave room for a real person to grow into it?

---

## 12. One-line principle

The best character prisms are not built from traits alone. They are built from a tension between how someone breaks, how they shine, and what it costs them to live in between — and they leave enough room that a real person can step inside and make that tension their own.
