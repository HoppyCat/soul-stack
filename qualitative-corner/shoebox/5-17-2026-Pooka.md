# What I Think Needs Attention

---

## 1. The Prose Is Often Over-Descriptive

This is the biggest craft issue.

You frequently:

* over-specify visual details
* stack adjectives
* explain emotional atmosphere twice
* linger too long on environmental rendering

Sometimes it works beautifully.

Other times it feels like:

> the prose doesn’t trust the reader enough.

For example:
you often describe:

* object
* texture
* lighting
* emotional implication
* symbolism

all at once.

That creates density fatigue.

The strongest moments are actually when you *simplify*.

Like:

> “The freedom from being yourself, or the freedom from being someone else?”

That line is fantastic because it’s clean.

Your worldbuilding becomes strongest when:

* detail serves emotional gravity
  instead of:
* detail proving imagination.

This is important if you want collaborative worldbuilding.

The tone sometimes swings between:

* mythic fantasy

* whimsical fairy-tale
* melodramatic romance

without always stabilizing the tonal contract.

That’s not automatically bad —

> tonal coherence boundaries.

Otherwise contributors won’t know:

* how serious the world is
* how emotionally grounded it is
* whether scenes should be ironic or sincere
* how dark consequences truly are

You need:

## tonal governance.

Not strict lore governance.

TONAL governance.

That’s actually more important.

> “If a tree falls in a forest, it DOES make a sound.”

Those are VERY different.

Ellie isn’t debating philosophy.
She’s defending reality itself.

Because to her, the alternative means:

* suffering can become unreal if ignored
* pain can disappear socially
* unseen people may as well not exist
* emotional abandonment rewrites reality
* silence invalidates experience

> “maybe it doesn’t,”

he accidentally threatens:

> the metaphysical legitimacy of her pain.

THAT’S why she explodes.

Not because she’s irrational —
but because the statement collides directly with:

* invisibility trauma
* emotional invalidation
* fear of being unheard
* fear of disappearing relationally

That’s MUCH deeper than “quirky recurring metaphor.”
