# Unicorn Tears Preview

## Prologue

*Kingdom of Diamonds*

You step into a quaint little shop, nestled in the heart of Catfish Cove. Shelves filled with glass bottles of all shapes and sizes, each containing a swirling, shimmering liquid, line the walls. The air is heavy with the scent of herbs and something else, something magical. In the center of the room, a wooden counter stands, bathed in the glow of a peculiar lamp. The lamp is a glass orb filled with bioluminescent plankton, suspended by a chain from the ceiling. The tiny creatures inside the orb emit a soft, ethereal light that dances across the room, casting an otherworldly glow on the figure behind the counter.

The door creaks shut behind you, sending a shiver down your spine. You steady yourself and approach the counter. The figure behind it is shrouded in a cloak of deep blue, their hood obscuring their face. Their hands, gloved in black leather, are folded on the counter, the only visible part of them. The rest is hidden in the shadows of the cloak.

You take a seat on the stool in front of the counter. The figure lifts their head slightly, revealing a mask. The mask is simple and made of porcelain, with intricate designs around the eyes, nose, and mouth. The light from the plankton lamp catches on the mask, creating an eerie play of light and shadow.

"Welcome," the figure says, their voice muffled by the mask. "What brings you to my humble shop?"

You clear your throat, trying to keep your voice steady. "I know you sell these novelty potions that can make a person become someone else for a day."

"That's correct," the figure replied. "Are you interested?”

“I’m interested in the potion that can turn you into someone else forever.”

“There’s a heavy price for that one,” the cloaked man warns sternly.

"I know," you reply. "Or, so I’ve been told."

The figure leaned back, the shadows deepening around them. "It's not just about money, you know. The one who sells these potions is bound to this place, unable to leave Catfish Cove or stop selling the potions until someone else willingly takes their place. Are you prepared for that?"

You swallow hard. You have a past you’d like to escape from, and could use a new start at a better life. But the thought of being bound to this place, of selling these potions, was daunting. It all sounded quixotic before standing here, in this place. But now you’re not so sure.

"Hmm?” The cloaked man leans forward towards you. He seems to be showing a keen sense of interest but trying not to show too much interest or else he might lose the sale.

“Will I remember my past?” you ask.

“Of course,” the figure reassures. “But if you prefer not to, there’s a potion for that.”

“Can I see the potion?”

“Aye,” the cloaked figure responds and reaches below the wooden counter to pick up a glass vial.

The vial itself appears to be a delicate piece of craftsmanship. It is made of iridescent glass that shimmers in the light, reflecting a myriad of colors that dance and change as it moves. The glass is slender and elegant, with a long neck that tapers to a small opening.

The stopper is a work of art in itself, made of polished silver that contrasts beautifully with the iridescent glass. It is intricately engraved with symbols and runes, their meanings known only to those who understand the magic contained within the vial.

Inside the vial, there are drops of a substance that looks akin to mercury. The substance moves with a life of its own, swirling and shifting within the confines of the glass. The potion is mesmerizing to watch, its movements almost hypnotic.

The sweet aroma from the vial is both enticing and comforting. It smells like a mixture of exotic spices and a hint of something floral.

“This vial is not just a container, but a key to a new life, a new identity. It holds the power to change you into someone else forever, but are you willing to pay the price?”

“...”

“You’d have to wait here like me for the next sale of it in order to be free. But I suppose one upside is that until someone takes your place, you basically live forever.”

“Now that’s interesting,” You catch yourself thinking out loud. Absolutely all of this sounds bonkers and you can’t quite remember how you arrived at this shop to ask this question. Did a friend challenge you to ask about the potion? Did you drink a bit too much? There’s definitely something in the air affecting your judgment right now.

You could head for the door. There’s nothing stopping you. Unless you wanted to go on a bit of an unknown adventure?

“So, then, what’s your decision? The freedom from being yourself, or the freedom from being someone else?”

## Chapter One

*Kingdom of Spades*

Princess Luciana ron Saley, Crown Princess of the Kingdom of Spades, woke with a gasp. Instinctively, she reached out towards her left for the strong and gentle hand of her husband, but her grasp found only empty sheets. “Leon?” she asked, turning to look. Her eyes confirmed what her heart already knew. Leon had dressed and gone.

Luciana climbed out of bed and stretched. She lit her bedside candle, then carried it over to her dressing table. The basin of cool water on the table was exactly what she needed to shock the nightmare from her mind. She splashed the water onto her face three times, then dried herself with a nearby towel.

Ignoring the heat she found on her forehead, Luciana strode to the window and pulled back the heavy shutters. Morning had not yet come. Even the sun’s daughters, the sparkling Vieri that wreathed the Lady of Morn, had not yet risen above the horizon to herald the arrival of their brilliant mother.

A deep twilight wreathed the grounds of Castle Veogar. The stronghold’s towers and turrets rose from the darkness in sparkles of torchlight like monuments to the dead. “Why up and gone so early, my love?” Luciana whispered into the air.

Her husband, Leon, was Crown Prince of the Kingdom of Spades. Following tradition, he was assigned the administration of Dalahar Province. His father had served in this castle prior to his coronation, as had his grandfather and so on, back a dozen generations. Leonel dar Theodahin, son of Leoric the Old, worked tirelessly to serve his subjects well.

Night after night, he came to their bed long after the moon was high in the sky. In the mornings, he rose with the Vieri, the Daughters of the Dawn. His desk was covered in papers, books, and folios filled with messages, correspondences, sums, inventories, and more. His pen was never dry. Thankfully, the same need not be said of his sword.

Folks as far as the Hinterlands knew the stories of Leon’s father. Leoric the Old was considered one of the most corrupt and ineffectual kings in the history of the Spades. He was the first king to lose a battle on Spadish soil, and that only to rebels. He was the first Spadish king to permit the Red Legion, the army of the Kingdom of Hearts, to enter Spadish territory, albeit under the pretense of peace.

But that only solidified his third greatest fault. Leoric had signed a defensive pact with the King of Hearts. Many councilors and administrators waved off the monumental implications of this near blasphemous document. It was just a formality, they said, putting on paper what had, for generations, been true in practice. But everyone knew that such an outright complacency with the power-hungry Kingdom of Hearts was an act of treason at best.

Luciana sighed and then strode over to her dressing table to change. Politics always frustrated her. She could never understand why leaders could not just do what was right, like Leon. Perhaps aristocrats are too distant from their citizens to have compassion for them?

Anyway, two subjects needed her full attention. No more distractions. Luciana covered her mouth, coughed, and placed her other hand to her chest. There was no room in the day’s schedule for sickness, so she determined herself to be in perfect health. Choosing not to be ill would be the easiest decision of the day.

She refocused. It would only take a few minutes to check on Alice. Seeing the Oracle, however, required a three-day ride to the capital and travel by Faerie’s Path to his dwelling at the Crossroads. The nightmare she just experienced was either an unwanted reminder of a long dead prophecy, or a message from the Oracle, himself – and Luciana already had a return message she was itching to give him.

*He’s going to take that curse right back or I’ll smash some of his precious bribes over his all-seeing forehead!* As a gift for his former prophecy, Leon and Luciana had presented the Oracle with a fabulous sword. Its blade was a solid piece of carved obsidian. Its grip was silk over ivory. Its quillons were gold and its pommel was encrusted with jewels. It was certainly the most fantastically elaborate, not to mention expensive, gift the Oracle had ever received, and if he is now haunting Luciana even in her sleep, she planned to jab it into his prescient eye-sockets.

That was enough thought of revenge and rebuke. Especially since it could have been just a dream and not a premonition. She frowned. There were more questions than answers.

Luciana finished dressing, draped her favorite pendant around her neck, and gave herself one more look in the mirror. She took in a deep breath and was calm again. The day was no brighter, save for the slightest crease of red on the horizon, but her mind was clear.

She saw in the mirror the visage of the queen she yearned to be. Strong yet gentle, serene, graceful and gracious.

Only the very best or the very worst royalty in the Kingdom of Spades received unique monikers. She did not know if she would ever receive one, but she always liked to think of herself as Luciana the Kind. She smiled at that, picked up her candle again, and turned to leave her bedchamber.

Moonlight fell upon the corridors of Castle Veogar. Pale silver streaks danced upon the art lining the walls and glimmered off the gilded spine of a dragon sculpture older than the castle in which it sat. The castle was quiet. Luciana always marveled at how silent the structure could be. The castle she grew up in was as chatty as a tea-toddler after his fifth cup of wine, all creeks and groans and tap-tap-tapping. She had grown up with those noises, and never feared them. But Veogar was as silent as a tomb, and she had a hard time ignoring the similarities.

Luciana walked down the corridor, pausing to cough once, twice, and then trekked through the castle to Alice’s bedchamber. After her nightmare, she had to see Alice with her own eyes. Luciana paused for a moment outside of Alice’s room. She stared at the finely carven door with picture stories etched into the wood. The carvings brought back memories of Luciana’s father, who loved to carve little figurines for his children as gifts. For a moment, Luciana clutched her heart and remembered how she used to hope she could be as good a parent as her own were. *If only my parents could see me now*.

Luciana opened the door. The dying fire cast ruddy shapes across the bed, the dressing table, the mirror, and an out of place statue. As Luciana approached the statue, her heart nearly stopped beating. The statue was definitely Alice – or rather, Alice was definitely the statue. She was dressed in her favorite nightgown, with her hair brushed down neatly for the night. Luciana felt the emptiness of the room like a hole in her own flesh. There was no presence there except her own in the room. Luciana trembled and began to panic. She stifled a scream. The door shut behind Luciana and she winced. Though the room was dim, it was lit well enough to see Alice’s face, and the fear in Alice’s eyes stole Luciana’s breath.

“Alice, can you hear me?” Luciana pleaded. Her voice shook. She tapped at the statue with her hands as she spoke. The statue did not move. “Alice, I don’t know what happened, but we’re going to help you.” Luciana fought with herself. Certainty flowed from her words but doubts flooded her heart. “Alice? Alice?!”   
	Luciana coughed again and began to make her way quickly out of the room. She didn’t want to leave her daughter, but she needed to get help. Her heart filled with terror wondering if Alice was alive, wondering if she could even breathe. She moved faster.

“She’s fine, you know,” said a creaky voice from behind, “Alice, I mean.”   
	Luciana turned and found Ms. Millie trundling down the hallway, pushing a little cart on wheels.

“And I’m about to brew some morning tea.”

“What could you possibly mean that she’s fine?”

“It’s a long story, my love. But unfortunately, we haven’t the time to talk, yet – We have company.”

“Company?” The sound of distant whispers fluttered down the hall. Luciana heard them clearly. As the whispers shifted into voices, she recognized one.

“Peter?” Luciana asked, peeking her head out from around the corner. Immediately, her mouth fell agape, and her eyes tensed. Luciana’s left hand involuntarily came to her mouth.

A little girl of eleven years — Luciana knew exactly how old she was — stood beside Peter holding a candle in a small brass dish. Messy strands of dark hair fell about her big green eyes, which shone in the candlelight. A thick surcoat of some foreign fashion with bird-shaped patches fell over a pair of bizarre hose with checker patterns. The girl wore a pair of white shoes with thick soles. The clothing was unlike anything Luciana had ever seen.

Fear and joy warred in Luciana’s heart. She found it difficult to breathe correctly and felt as if she might swoon. The girl met Luciana’s gaze. There was no recognition in her eyes which filled Luciana’s stomach with the most bitter pangs of grief.

The girl dropped her gaze. Her discomfort snapped Luciana out of her rumination. Luciana grasped for control of her emotions. She was vaguely aware that Peter was talking. After a moment, she regained her composure. Then lost it again.

Peter's voice echoed, concern lacing his words. “Your highness?” he called out. “Are you alright?”

Luciana turned to face him, her face pale in the candlelight.

“Have you taken ill?” Peter asked, his eyes filled with worry.

“No!” Luciana's response was sharper than she intended, causing Peter to take a step back. She took a calming breath, her gaze dropping to the flickering candle in her hand before meeting Peter's eyes again. “I’m sorry,” she said, her voice softer. “I’ve not slept well, but I am listening. Go on.”

Peter nodded, hesitating for a moment before speaking again. “There is a statue in your daughter’s room... We believe it is Princess Alice.”

“She’s...” Peter's voice trailed off, his gaze dropping to the floor. “Turned to stone, I think.” He paused, swallowing hard before continuing. “When I entered her room, I saw a pooka fairy with her stone statue, but he disappeared before I could question him.”

“We’ll need to find that fairy,” Luciana said, her voice steady despite the fear gnawing at her. “But first, I have one question.”

“What is that, your highness?” Peter asked, his eyes meeting hers.

Luciana's gaze landed on the girl accompanying Peter. Gathering her strength, she smiled and asked, “You’re Ellie, right?”

Ellie nodded and returned the smile.

“Alice talks about you often,” Luciana continued.

“She does?” Ellie asked, her eyes lighting up.

Luciana nodded, fighting back tears. “Before I join you both, I have a matter that requires my immediate attention.” Luciana explained. “In the meantime, Peter, I need you to find the pooka you saw.”

Peter nodded.

“Yes,” Luciana continued. “It won’t be far. Ellie, would you mind assisting Peter in the search for the creature?”

“You mean Bubbles?” Ellie asked.

Luciana, suppressing her curiosity about the pooka, simply asked, “Bubbles?”

“Yes, Bubbles. That’s what Alice named him,” Ellie replied.

Luciana smiled briefly, then said, “Yes, will you help Peter look for Bubbles?”

Ellie nodded. “Sure!”

“Thank you.” Then to Peter she said, “Be careful and be kind. We’re asking for help, not taking a prisoner.”

“Yes, your highness,” Peter said.

“Thank you,” Luciana said to them both. They went off.

Ms. Millie disappeared into a cloud of black smoke before Luciana could finish her sentence. The word, “Luciana,” came from within the miasma, echoing somehow. Luciana squinted and blinked, and her childhood friend Mr. Fluffy appeared; not as a small bunny rabbit, but as a tall, dark-furred pooka. The long, fuzzy ears, the fiery orange eyes, the whiskers... Luciana, unable to withhold the grin on her face, soaked in every feature of the pooka. She could barely do more but hold herself back from standing up and taking the creature into a big hug.

It had been eleven years since she last saw Mr. Fluffy, and a little less time since Ms. Millie appeared at the castle gates. The elderly stranger offered her services to Leon and Luciana in exchange for housing and meals. On an ordinary day, such an offer would be dismissed. But something about that day felt out of the ordinary – it was a day much like this, where nothing felt predictable or impossible.

“I tried to go home...” the pooka’s voice was exactly as Luciana remembered. It was gruffer, perhaps older, but filled with a sweet sincerity. Mr. Fluffy searched Luciana’s eyes as he continued, “But once my family found out what I did...”

“You were banished from Fairy’s Shoal?”

“I didn’t know where else to go but here,” Mr. Fluffy sniffed. His eyes filled with tears. “You were my only other family.”

Luciana leaned forward, “You were banished for swapping the girls?” She spoke the question so quietly it was almost a whisper.

Suddenly, a crash in the hallway distracted both parties, followed by a brief, “Ellie!” from Peter and an “I’m so sorry!” from Ellie. Their bickering voices faded down the hall as the children argued who was at the greater fault for the damages. The sound of the children drew Luciana’s attention to March, who was working nearby in Luciana’s solar room to swap out Luciana’s cooling cup of tea with something hot and freshly brewed.

March Timrin, Alice’s future lady in waiting, came from the westernmost point of the kingdom where the fisher folk have a distinct dialect. To March’s chagrin, Luciana knew, the child had not been able to fully scrape the low-born accent from her speech and it came out sometimes.

Luciana couldn’t help but smile. March was a perfect little lady. In such times that Alice had made a mess or acted unladylike Luciana had wondered if the girl raised to be princess would ever be able to match the low-born March’s grace. The only thing Alice had over March was her accent, or lack thereof.

Gravity returned to the conversation.

Mr. Fluffy nodded, “Aye, your highness.”

“I’m sorry,” Luciana said. “But I need your help.” Luciana gathered her courage and leaned forward. “I can’t change the past. But Alice is in trouble and I don’t know how to help her.”

Mr. Fluffy’s ears sagged for a moment.

“Do you remember when you sent me away with your daughter?”

Luciana held her head. Her mind was so foggy. Just keeping focus required all the effort she could muster. But she would never forget the day she gave her daughter away.

“I do. I remember it every day.”

“You asked me to find a home for her, somewhere that she would be safe from the Oracle’s prophecy.”

“I used to think any mother would do the same.”

“Because I loved you, I searched the galaxies for the best world for your child.”

Tears welled up in Luciana’s eyes as Mr. Fluffy continued.

“I searched far and wide for the perfect home, and then I found an infant who looked similar enough to your daughter; hardly any hair on her head, and quite cute and chunky. She was the youngest of four or five children. The house was worn and tired and so seemed the parents, but I saw no evidence that the infant was unloved.”

Luciana was in a trance listening to Mr. Fluffy’s tale.

“She was alone in her own room and wrapped in the blanket I told you about. The one with her name hand stitched onto it, ‘Alice,’ which in her world, means, ‘noble.’”

The story trance momentarily healed Luciana of her cough. She had also forgotten about the fresh tea March had fixed for her, which was cooling in her solar.

“What happened next?” she asked.

“It was meant to be a protection spell and nothing more,” Mr. Fluffy continued. “My plan was to protect the girls from catching illnesses from each other’s worlds. I left some treasures that could be sold for food and supplies for the infant. Certainly, with the gold you insisted upon me, the family could afford supplies for two newborn girls.”

Luciana nodded. It all made sense so far. And answered a lot of questions she had throughout the years; as Mr. Fluffy disappeared without an explanation after leaving the new infant back with Luciana and Leon.

“But something happened when I spoke the spell. I don’t know what it was, but it horrified even me. Right before my eyes, Princess Alice turned into stone.”

Gulping down hard, Luciana whispered, “Alice...”

“Yes.” Mr. Fluffy looked down. “I thought she was dead. I thought I- I thought perhaps I killed her. She was so tiny and defenseless. I couldn’t stomach that I could do such a thing.”

“What happened, then?” Luciana felt she could burst.

“Alice had turned into stone, as did her clothes, but her blanket had not. It was strange. It was almost as if it was left for your child.” Mr. Fluffy lifted his head and locked eyes with Luciana. “I wrapped your daughter in the blanket and placed her gently next to the stone newborn.”

“I see,” Luciana said, “Go on.”

“I did the only thing I thought I could do. I brought the stone infant back to our world.”

“When I returned to this world,” Mr. Fluffy continued, “The babe returned to life. The stone exterior cracked away and there she was: a perfectly healthy, crying baby.”

“Wait, so that’s why you said Alice is okay?” Luciana’s heart rose and fell while her stomach lurched. This was all too much, too fast. “Which means they can’t be in the same world at the same time...”

“Without one of them turning to stone, it seems,” Mr. Fluffy completed Luciana’s spoken thought as he tried to sort out the logic, himself.

Peter and Ellie turned the corner and saw them in the hallway. Peter’s mouth was agape staring at the gigantic pooka having a chat with Luciana.

Peter closed his mouth, crossed his arms and kept his expression neutral, or at least, he tried to. Ellie stood next to Mr. Fluffy, already asking a million questions.

Luciana joined them. Ellie looked up at her.

“What’s next?” Ellie asked.

“I’m sorry, dear one, but we have to send you back,” replied Luciana.

## Chapter Two

*The Crossroads*

Declan traversed the magical gateway that bridged the Crossroads and the Faerie’s Path.

“Sir,” said the Gatekeeper, a portly Heart with a chinstrap beard and a bald head, “Do you have the necessary documents to use the Faerie Path?”

Declan, dressed in a dusty green tunic and faded blue cloak, put his hand in his saddlebag and sensed the sudden tension of two dozen crossbows, all drawn and loaded. He sneered at the guardsmen positioned above. Down on his level, four halberdiers in heavy armor lowered their weapons.

Declan raised his other hand to show he meant no trouble, and then drew the papers he himself had signed and sealed and handed them to the gatekeeper. The portly man took, opened and read them.

He turned toward the halberdiers and waved them off. The four men relaxed. Their weapons resumed an upright position, poised to strike at any sudden moves from Declan.

Declan couldn’t help but smile. A smudge of dirt on his face, a change of clothes, and the absence of jewelry rendered Declan practically unrecognizable, to the point that four of his own soldiers were prepared to skewer him like a wild boar.

The gatekeeper folded the papers back up and handed them to Declan.

“You’re free to go, sir,” he said.

Declan took the papers, deposited them back in his saddlebags and then cued his horse to a walk. As he passed them by he offered the most flourishing and disrespectful salute he could muster. The guardsmen eyed from under the raised faceplates of their helms but said nothing. They had perfect discipline.

These men need a raise, Declan thought.

The streets were sparsely populated, with only the occasional merchant or scullery maid out and about at this hour. Declan thundered past them.

He halted at a small stable adjacent to the postern gate of the Oracle’s mansion, entrusting the reins of his horse to a man with a gooseneck who reeked of moldy cheese. After paying him, he drew his cloak around himself and headed up to the wall of the castle.

Declan took the stairs that hugged the wall up to the little postern gate, a single doorway not wide enough for two men to walk through even if they squeezed. Declan looked up and saw the shadow of a man at the top of the nearby tower. Then he beat on the door until his hand was numb.

“For the love of mercy, who is it?” A gargling voice called from behind.

“I come on behalf of my lady,” Declan called back. “Her eyes have gone dark as the crow.”

"There came no reply, only the scraping sound of wood and iron moving. Then the door opened. Declan stepped in. The man with the gargly voice shut the door behind him, then turned and squinted his beady eyes at Declan. His voice might have been his best quality. The man was thin and wiry, except for a round belly. His face was subtly swine-like, with big, flaring nostrils and pink skin. Declan threw his cloak back, revealing the old sword at his side.

"You'll have to leave that here," the man said, revealing a missing tooth as he spoke. He gestured at Declan's sword with a coiled whip.

Declan unbuckled the baldric, letting the sword and belt fall to the ground, stirring up a cloud of dust.

"Let's go!"

Declan followed the man, who reminded him of a pig, through back courtyards and alleys. The Oracle's mansion was a complex of small buildings surrounding a massive keep and donjon. Most of these buildings were dark, but a few were lit. As he passed a smithy, Declan could hear the familiar ring of metal on metal. Servants passed him here and there, their faces sleepy and vacant.

Finally, the man led him through a back door into the keep and up some stairs.

"Where are you leading me?" Declan asked.

The man ignored him.

"This is not the way to the Oracle's study," Declan said.

"You're not seeing the Oracle today. Such a meeting would be too noticeable," the man huffed. It was difficult to tell if the man was huffing to be snide or because he was out of shape. After a few more stairs, he glanced back and continued, "And we wouldn't want people knowing you've come to see him, would we, your majesty?" The man snorted as he said the last two words.

Declan gritted his teeth. He felt his hands squeezing into fists. What was the likelihood that this loose-lipped fool would keep his secret?

All of this back-room dealing is for nothing if he blows my cover, Declan thought.

“How much is the Oracle paying you to keep your mouth shut regarding my identity?” Declan asked.

“Maybe he expects you to do that,” the piggish man snorted.

Wrong answer, Declan thought. He flicked his right wrist and then caught the dagger that fell from the hidden holster.

“There’s only one problem with that,” Declan said casually, trying to hide the sudden surge of energy he felt. “I don’t pay off loose ends.”

Declan moved. Left hand grabbing the piggish man’s shirt. Right hand bringing the dagger to his throat. Right foot smashing into the back of the man’s knee. The piggish man fell back into Declan’s hold.

“Okay, your... er, sir,” the piggish man managed to gargle. His voice was so high there was barely any breath in it, just a wet whisper. “I was only joking! ...Joking!”

Declan squeezed his hold on the man tighter.

"The Oracle pays me well and has entrusted many secrets to me," the piggish man blurted after a pause to cough. “Never once have I betrayed him. Never once. Not even under pain of torture. I swear it, I swear it!”

Declan maintained his grip on the man, surveying his every expression as he squirmed.

"Your secret is safe with me," the man said between panicked breaths. "I’ll take it to my grave."

Declan could see the fear that widened the piggish man’s beady eyes. Taking long, slow breaths, he worked to calm himself, signaling to his mind and body that there was no more need for danger.

Declan pushed the piggish man back to his feet and released him. He returned his blade to its hidden sheath and rearmed the release mechanism, though he was sure he wouldn’t need to use it again.

"Just a little further, sir," the man said, and Declan followed.

Finally, the man with piggish features halted at a heavy, iron-strapped door high in the keep. He bowed and gestured towards the door.

“The Oracle's Voice awaits within, sir,” he said.

“He certainly loves his pomp, this Oracle," Declan remarked. He opened the door and stepped inside.

He found himself in a large storage room. Boxes, crates, and various items shrouded in tarps lined the walls to his right, left, and behind him, leaving only a small rectangle of empty space in the center. This space was dominated by a simple desk, flanked by chairs on either side, underneath an unusual hanging light. The light was an assembly of several lit candles over a bowl of water, all suspended by thin chains. The water seemed to magnify the candlelight, casting a cone of warm light onto the desk and the masked man seated behind it.

As the heavy door shut behind him, Declan felt a shiver of fear run down his spine. He didn't let it show. Instead, he sauntered to the desk and took a seat in the nearest chair.

The mask the stranger wore featured deep, curving forms around the eyes, nose, and mouth, which caught the light from the lamp above, creating bizarre shapes of light and darkness. The man’s black leather-clad hands were casually clasped on the desk. His eyes were hidden in the shadows of the frightful mask. Any other features were hidden by a black cloak.

Declan sat, inspecting the stranger for any telltale sign of his identity or intentions. For all he knew, the stranger was doing the same. After a few moments of watching the stranger sit motionless, Declan kicked his chair back onto its hind legs and slapped his dusty boots onto the desk.

“That’s unnecessary, Your Majesty,” the stranger said. Declan’s breath caught. The voice did not sound muffled, as one would expect from someone speaking through a mask; instead, it seemed to emanate from the mask itself. It was deep and cold as the grave.

Declan instinctively put his feet back on the floor and straightened his chair, then hated himself for showing fear to some freak in a mask.

“Fascinating,” Declan said, attempting to mask his fear with sarcasm. “Your mask has some kind of magical properties, right?”

The masked man remained silent and motionless.

“So it both amplifies and alters your voice,” Declan continued. “Which actually,” he added, “manages to counteract the intended effect. You wouldn’t need to alter your voice unless I knew you.” He stroked his dirty chin with one hand.

“So... I would say that you are—”

“I am bored of this,” the Voice snapped. Declan’s mouth clenched shut.

“The Oracle has ordered me to impart valuable information to you,” the Voice said, his tone cold and full of judgment.

“Let’s hear it,” Declan replied, shrugging nonchalantly.

“He is disappointed that you have chosen to break ties with him.”

“Well, his services are expensive and often fruitless,” Declan retorted. “The Kingdom of Hearts has flourished without his so-called assistance.”

Ignoring Declan's comment, the Voice continued, “And you chose to disregard his advice regarding your selection of a queen.”

“Which is an excellent example of my prior point,” Declan said. “Renata has proven herself a worthy queen-”

“Yet you are unhappy with her,” the Voice interjected, his voice laden with mockery. “You desire another. Is that not correct?”

“What can I say?” Declan said, lifting his hands in a gesture of surrender, “I am a man of great desires. That doesn’t make Renata unfit for her crown.”

“Her next son will not be yours,” the Voice said. “The Oracle has foreseen it.”

“And has the Oracle foreseen how much I will owe him for ruining that fancy mask when I-” Declan's voice rose to a shout, “shove a blade through it?”

Declan stood abruptly, kicking the chair away from him, and triggered the release on the hidden sheath. The dagger slid perfectly into his hand. The Voice did not move.

“It is not up to me,” the Voice said, “what the Oracle sees. I am only the Voice and I speak what is spoken to me.”

“You really are who I think you are, aren’t you?” Declan asked.

“Please,” the Voice said, pointing at Declan’s upended chair. “Sit.”

Declan eyed the masked figure for a moment longer before sheathing his dagger and righting the chair. Once seated, the Voice resumed speaking.

“The Oracle has chosen to forgive these offenses,” the Voice intoned, “and will, in exchange for a few concessions on your part, honor the promise made to you eleven years ago. If-”

“If?” Declan asked, feigning ignorance despite knowing exactly what the Voice referred to.

“Eleven years ago,” the Voice recounted, “The Oracle spoke of a unique and powerful item he might one day ask you to retrieve.”

The memory was vivid in Declan’s mind—the Oracle’s words and the visions that had accompanied them.

“I recall,” Declan admitted, his voice devoid of its earlier sarcasm.

“The Oracle now requests your services to recover this item,” the Voice stated, its tone betraying a hint of eagerness.

“So, he’s confirmed it then,” Declan mused. “He wants me to find this artifact for him?”

“Yes,” the Voice affirmed.

“And if I agree?”

“The Oracle’s promise remains intact.”

Declan’s interest piqued. “And these concessions?”

The Voice hesitated before replying, “The Oracle does not seek your separation from Renata. What he desires, I believe you will find quite reasonable. You must understand, however, you are not the only one enlisted in this quest for the artifact.”

A serious look crossed Declan's face. “That seems reasonable,” he said. “A good Heart isn’t threatened by a little competition.”

“Where’s the danger in agreeing, then?”

Declan nodded, mentally filing away this piece of information for future consideration. “And the other concession? Is what?” he asked, his words coming out slowly.

The Voice did not reply. Instead, he turned and opened a drawer in the desk, pulled something out, and set it on the table.

Declan recognized the item immediately. There, on the table, sat a small crystal vial of clear fluid. The stopper was sealed to the bottle with red wax. He picked up the bottle and brought it closer to his face. He gently wiggled the bottle and the clear liquid sloshed around. It could have been water save for the pale white glow it gave off as it moved.

Declan looked up at the Voice, peering hard into the shadowy eye slits. But there was nothing—only an impenetrable darkness.

*If it means getting what I want*, Declan thought, *I’d make a deal with the Jabberwock*.

“Okay,” Declan said, pocketing the vial. “How do I find this artifact?”

He could feel, rather than see, that the Voice of the Oracle was smiling.

## Chapter Three

*Kingdom of Clovers*

"Anselm,” Cheshire shouted over the frigid winds roaring down from the mountain, “move your ass!”

“His name is Norbert,” Anselm shouted back, pointing to the donkey that was now fighting his lead tooth and hoof.

“I refuse to call him that,” Cheshire shouted.

He and Anselm had been traveling from Haven for weeks, but only began their mountain ascent three days ago.

They followed the Sardisian Rift, a wide gorge that separated the Jabberspine Mountains and the Southwall Ridge mountains. Towering above them stood the Oracle’s Peak to the right and Ironhead Mountain to the left. Between these massive bodies, the canyon turned into a narrow gorge endlessly buffeted by winds despite its concealed nature.

Cheshire hated travel and he hated traveling through the gorge even more. He wasn’t made for this kind of activity. His brain wallowed in boredom every second he was away from his books. Additionally, the high altitude made it freezing cold. The layers of wool, leather and fur helped none at all.

“Why don’t we just take the Fairy Road to Corvix Bero?” Anselm had asked weeks ago when Cheshire was back at Shepherd’s Palace in Haven, tucked into his warm chair surrounded by his beloved books.

It was a valid question, but Cheshire had opted against the quicker, safer, and less mountainous route due to its inherent dangers.

“Because I’m not a fool,” Cheshire had responded. “Taking the road towards the Heartian capital would bring too much suspicion. If I’m right, I need to get there in secret.”

Cheshire had been very secretive. He had avoided the Fairy Road even just from Clover’s capital to the Crossroads so not to fall under the curious gaze of the Oracle. He even avoided making an appearance in Court in order to keep his information away from spies. Instead, during their usual evening game of stones, he had privately asked King Oberyn for permission to investigate a matter of great importance.

“Why would you need my leave to go anywhere?” the King had said. As he did so he placed a stone white-side up on the board between them, and then flipped the black-side-up stones he had flanked.

“Because I’m going into the Kingdom of Hearts.” Cheshire put his own stone, black-side-up into the corner, flipping four white stones to black.

“And do you mind if I ask why?” King Oberyn showed no concern in his voice as he asked, only curiosity. He grabbed another stone and flipped it in his fingers revealing white, then black, then white again, over and over. At last he set it down on the board and flipped two more stones. Oberyn wasn’t very good at stones.

“I can’t tell you yet,” Cheshire replied. He set another stone down, black-side-up and flipped three.

King Oberyn picked up another stone, looked at the board and sighed. He had only one legal move left, after which Cheshire would claim the game. So he set the stone back down and looked Cheshire in the eyes. “You’re not an agent in my employ, Cheshire, and you can go anytime or anywhere you please.”

“I know.” Cheshire returned the stones to the sides of the board for a second game. “But if I don’t come back, I need someone to give this to.” He pulled a thick scroll, sealed in green wax and emblazoned with his personal emblem, from his pocket.

The king took it and looked at it. He felt the weight of it and then looked back at Cheshire.

“What’s this?”

“That,” Cheshire said, “is a copy of my notes on this latest project, as concise as I can make them. If I don’t come back within two months, please open and read it.”

Oberyn’s eyes narrowed. He took one more look at the hefty scroll and then back at Cheshire. The firelight reflected in his eyes. “Are you in trouble?”

“Not yet.” Cheshire sat back and took a drink from the wine that had been lingering on the side table. He met Oberyn’s gaze. “But if I’m right, and the wrong people find out, this will be the last time I stomp you in stones.”

“You’re leaving soon, then,” Oberyn said.

“I am, tomorrow morning.”

“Anselm,” Cheshire screamed back to the boy, “let’s make camp!” The sun had limped behind the Ironhead and the gorge was cast into gloomy twilight. He dismounted.

Anselm rode up beside him, still holding the lead of the donkey and looked down at him. All Cheshire could see of his thirteen year old apprentice was a red-cheeked face and a few curls of dark hair behind the furs. “Shouldn’t we go on a few more miles? It’s not too dark yet.”

“I’m frozen to my bones,” Cheshire said back. Well, he shouted it back. It was difficult to hold any kind of conversation in such confounded wind. “Besides, I want to check the maps and notes one more time. If we’re lucky, tomorrow will find us at our destination.”

The rocky crevasse was pocked with little cavities and hollows. Cheshire handed Anselm the reins and, after a little looking, found one suitable for them.

“In here!” Cheshire said.

Anselm nodded and they went to work. The boy started by driving a piton into a rock in another nearby hollow and then tying the animals to it. He unsaddled the horses, lay the heavy blankets over them and put food bags to their faces. Cheshire drove pitons into the rock above and below their own little shelter and then fitted a heavy tarp to them to block the wind.

Anselm unpacked the provisions, equipment and the few maps and manuscripts they could bring while Cheshire lit a fire. Their little den had the perfect shape in a ceiling, which angled the smoke up and out through the small gap in the tarp. After about a half an hour of work, mostly Anselm’s, the two were snug in a relatively warm cave with a hot meal simmering on the fire. Cheshire sat in a bundle of furs atop his sleeping bag and read over one of the maps he had brought.

After a few moments Anselm brought a steaming bowl of something to Cheshire and then, filling his own bowl, sat across the fire and began to eat. Their shelter stood quiet for the next several minutes. Anselm had no work to do at the moment, so he ate quietly and casually read one of the manuscripts they’d brought.

Cheshire read, taking occasional spoonfuls of the meal, which turned out to be some kind of soup consisting mostly of beans. Anselm would be a fine scholar someday, but never a very good cook. Perhaps next time Cheshire picked an apprentice, he would find an idiot who could make a decent meal out of anything. That apprentice would never make much of himself, at least not in academia, but he might, at least, make Cheshire’s travels more comfortable.

“What does this mean, anyway?” Anselm asked. Cheshire took another swallow of the soup and looked up. Anselm held up the manuscript and pointed. Even in noon-day light Cheshire wouldn’t have been able to read it.

“What?” he asked.

“Lexo Durum,” Anselm read aloud. “The best I can tell it looks like ‘king’s blood,’ or ‘king’s doom’ but the syntax is backwards, so it actually reads ‘blood’s king’ or ‘doom’s king.’”

Cheshire laughing a dry chuckle.

“What?” Anselm asked.

“Those early Hearts loved their associative language.”

“Yeah,” Anselm said, scratching his head. “I guess. Protolucian languages don’t make any sense to me.”

“You see, the language, as I said, was highly associative. Words came from other words which came from other words, with very slight alterations. For example, the word for castle or fortress comes from their word for crown, since a round crown represents the fortification a king encircles himself with. And, of course, the word for crown comes from king. *Lexo* doesn’t refer to a king. That would be lek. And it doesn’t refer to crown as that would be let. So, Lexo refers to...”

“Fortress,” Anselm said. “So it’s blood’s fortress or doom’s fortress?”

“Actually,” Cheshire said, “the ‘*um*’ likely refers more to preposition than possession.”

“So we’re going to the fortress of blood or the fortress of doom?” Anselm’s voice squeaked as he spoke, which he worked to hide by clearing his throat.

“That’s exactly where we’re going.”

Two days later the Sardisian Rift widened up again and lay before Cheshire as a wide rocky valley. Here the gales subsided. In that valley, maybe ten miles ahead and a few hundred feet below Cheshire could see the very thing he’d come all this way to find: a massive crumbling fortress. It was only a wisp of orange against the dark evening sky, but its shadow would weigh heavily on them both now that they had to camp so close. But, at the same time, the idea that the place existed, now a reality, would keep him awake all night with anticipation.

They made camp once more. Anselm appeared to sleep after their meal of bland bean soup, but Cheshire lay awake in his sleeping bag, staring at the stony roof of their little hollow as the waning firelight cast angry red images against its jagged surface.

“Master?”

Something grabbed Cheshire’s shoulder and shook him. But that didn’t make any sense. He was already awake, sipping tea in his study in Shepherd’s Palace as he read a manuscript of... something. Now that he thought of it, he couldn’t really make sense of what he was reading. He *seemed* to read but as soon as he looked at the page it was fuzzy.

“Master!”

He was shaken again and all was darkness.

“Master, wake up!”

Cheshire opened his eyes and then instantly regretted it. The sunlight flooded his sight. Cheshire grunted and squeezed his eyes. “What time is it?”

“It’s about eight o’ clock,” Anselm said. “It seemed like you didn’t close your eyes until late in the night, so I let you sleep a few extra hours.

Cheshire sighed. Eight o’ clock. He’d lost two unrecoverable hours to his greatest weakness: the need for sleep. He shaded his eyes with a hand and then tried to open them again. They stung like salt on a wound, but he forced them to stay open until tears formed in the corners. Then he stopped, an icy spike of panic going down his spine. “Did you go outside?”

“Yes, to feed the animals.”

“Did you see... it?” There was a pause. Cheshire, still blinded by his weary, stinging eyes, couldn’t tell if Anselm was confused or just pausing for effect.

“It’s right there where we left it,” Anselm said.

Cheshire sighed again, this time in relief. He hadn’t imagined the fortress. He struggled out of his sleeping bag and furs. He grabbed his wineskin and squeezed much of the water onto his face. It was frigid.

“Master, are you sure you don’t want to get a few more hours of sleep?”

Cheshire shivered and blew out with his mouth, and then grabbed a towel to dry his face. “I do my best work on an hour of sleep.” He tried to sound suave and not at all as mentally and physically drained as he felt. Cheshire stood on his feet and staggered out into the blinding sunlight. He took a few steps out into the valley, and, blinking away at the stinging and the tears, he felt as if he might fall down at any step; but he didn’t fall.

Cheshire turned and saw once more the fortress that the ancient Hearts had called Lexo Durum and which the first Spades might have called Doomholt. Even though he saw it in full morning light, it looked even more foreboding, seeing as the sun was behind it, making it a dark silhouette against the blue sky. He took a few deep breaths to invigorate and revive him more.

He was tired from too much travel, too much cold and only the few hours of sleep he had, but against that weariness fought his rising anxiety, putting trembling strength in his limbs and a hot edge to his mind.

Cheshire turned back at Anselm, who was ready for the excursion. He was dressed and fed and on his back was a small pack of the things they might need for their adventure. Aware of the potential dangers lurking inside the shadowy ruin, he was nonetheless willing, perhaps even eager, to embark.

“Are you sure you want to come?” Cheshire asked.

“I am,” Anselm said. “I’ve got everything we might need.” Taking a shaky breath but striving to appear brave, he said, “The horses are fed and saddled. I’ve loosened Norbert's line so he can escape if we don’t return.”

“Good man,” Cheshire said. “Let’s go.”

They mounted up and Anselm followed Cheshire down the widening rocky valley towards the great shadow ahead. As they drew closer Cheshire attempted to make a mental map of it. So far as he could see, the fortress consisted of a large keep that sat atop what looked like a massive earthwork that had been squared off behind a retaining wall. Surrounding the central keep stood two concentric rings of walls regularly bisected with tall, square towers. These concentric walls appeared to be octagonal in shape but Cheshire would need to do a lot of surveying before he could make that conclusion for the entire fortress.

After an hour of slow riding, they came upon the fortress’s great yawning gatehouse, its two extending towers flanking the open maw left and right. Cheshire scanned the battlements above but saw nothing move. The great doors had fallen and had mostly rotted away, their hinges rusted beyond repair. The portcullis hung in shambles above them as they passed. Cheshire peered through the murder holes above but again saw nothing move. Buildings sat in hopeless disrepair. Their thatch roofs had long caved in and their plaster walls had warped, crumbled and fallen to leave behind their half-timber skeletons. In many places the battlements had fallen away. The walls were still solid but here and there the merlons and walkways above had fallen prey to ages of disuse and the elements and simply crumbled.

Warfare was not Cheshire’s specialty, nor was siegecraft, but even he could tell this fortress was masterfully designed. Once inside the exterior wall, he and Anselm had to ride to the far side to get to the next gate, and around a few corners that would serve too tight a squeeze for a battering ram, and all the while being peppered by arrows from above. Interior towers and exterior towers were staggered in position so the defenders always had a superior position.

The place had clearly been left behind technologically. The defenses on the walls were flat battlements and had no machicolations or other ways to easily beset attackers standing directly underneath them. Likewise none of the walls or towers had a sloping base to keep siege towers at bay. But despite all of the defensive flaws, time itself caused the collapse of the castle.

Cheshire rode through the second gatehouse and found himself dwarfed by the massive keep on its huge earthwork. As he approached it, Cheshire became aware of a sound unlike any he’d ever heard. A deep rumbling seemed to come from the keep, rising and falling.

He could see the remains of a manpowered crane system that might have served as a lift for bringing goods and people to the keep more easily, but the lift cage was already at the top and Cheshire saw no way of bringing it down, let alone raising it back in place. That left the only way to the keep a thin staircase that wound back and forth against the retaining wall, up to the base of the keep.

“That’s a lot of climbing,” Anselm said.

Cheshire nodded. “The horses will have to stay here.” He looked back to Anselm. “Can you rig their leads the same way you did the donkey’s?”

Anselm looked at Cheshire. There was a glint of pale fear in his eyes, but then he visibly steeled himself and nodded. He led Cheshire to an old row of trees. There he tied the horses down with a loose knot.

They climbed.

Cheshire tried to count the steps as he went up them but lost count somewhere around fifty. He added another fifty even though he was sure he was only on eighty or so. Then he lost count again. By the time he reached the top after several breaks, which he spent mostly holding onto the wall while he struggled for air, he came to a liberal number of two hundred steps.

“How far... did we climb?” asked Anselm as he huffed and puffed.

Attempting to straighten up and appear tough, Cheshire ended up coughing and doubling over again, panting with his hands on his knees. “I expect under two hundred steps.” He gasped for air and continued. “They were pretty tall, say ten inches on average which means we climbed about a hundred and seventy feet.”

“Felt more like five hundred,” Anselm replied through gulps of air.

“You’re too young... to be so out of breath,” Cheshire said.

“Does that mean I get to call you an ‘old man,’ then?”

“Choose your next words carefully.”

After a few more minutes of catching their breath, Cheshire straightened and surveyed the keep. It stood about four stories tall with great windows topped with cinquefoil arches. Great buttresses protruded from its walls and the corners bulged outward into turrets. Once again, though, nothing moved on the battlements above. The sound of that sonorous humming noise was stronger here. Cheshire could more clearly make out the rising and falling actions.

“What is that sound?” Anselm asked.

“Doom,” Cheshire replied.

Anselm blinked.

“Let’s go,” Cheshire said.

He led Anselm across the large, flat stones to the keep, which had its own smaller gatehouse, and entered through the broken doors. Cheshire found himself in a large, two-story room. A catwalk on the second story sported crenelated battlements similar to the exterior of the fortress. Three doorways opened out: left, right and forward.

Cheshire went forward. Anselm followed. The corridor was just as defensively devised as the entry room. The ceiling was covered in murder holes. The walls had thin slits from which defenders could loose arrows.

“It’s so strange,” Anselm whispered against the thrumming sound that emanated from everywhere. Cheshire turned and saw his apprentice looking around, the gears and belts of his intellect turning. “This is truly just a fortress.”

“How do you mean?” Cheshire whispered back as they entered a side chamber. This one still had murder holes in the ceiling. An ancient table and some chairs sat in a heap in the middle of the room covered in dust. They seemed sturdily crafted but by no means luxurious.

“Most castles,” Anselm replied as they stepped back out into the hallway, “well, *all* castles are living spaces. If I owned a castle, yes, I would build up its defenses but, on the inside, I would make it a comfortable place. Considering that I could afford such a work of architecture, I would panel the walls, put rugs on the floors and fill it with pieces of art. I would, you know, make it comfortable.”

“But there’s no evidence of that here,” Cheshire said, finishing Anselm’s thought. They stepped into another chamber. This one was a wide room. In the center, a massive iron chandelier had smashed into a long table, breaking it in two and sending some of the table’s legs, not to mention several of the chairs, off in every direction. Now all was coated in dust. Cheshire saw something there on the floor at the epicenter of the crash and headed over to it.

“It’s almost as if this entire massive place is just to keep people out,” Anselm said. “No lord or king actually lived here. And a much more conservative fortification would dissuade a southern attacker from trekking the Sardisian Rift.”

“Yes,” Cheshire said, stepping over to the thing that attracted his eye. It was a hole. “Look here.”

Anselm stepped over to Cheshire and looked.

“It’s a hole,” he said. “So?”

Cheshire shook his head.

“So, we’re on the bottom floor of this keep. The earthwork the founders built this on must be solid stone.”

“Maybe they dug a basement?”

Cheshire walked around the table and put his hands on the chandelier.

“Help me move this,” he said. Anselm shrugged and stepped up. The two grunted and groaned, and finally they turned the chandelier over, further crushing the table and filling the room with a crashing noise.

“Jabberwock’s eye,” Anselm cursed, “the queen of Diamonds probably heard that!”

Cheshire moved back across to Anselm’s side and the two began pushing furniture away from the hole, no longer concerning themselves with stealth. If there were enemies here, they would be coming whether they made noises with the furniture or not. Then Cheshire got down on his hands and knees and peered through the crack.

“What do you see?” Anselm asked. Cheshire strained to look, to make sense of anything - but all he saw was darkness. His eyes began to play tricks on him, projecting forms against the darkness that soon disappeared. One thing he knew for sure, the noise he’d heard was louder than ever. A deep rumbling bellowed through the crack at him. Then a low, grinding hiss.

Cheshire shook his head.

“Darkness,” he said. Anselm then asked another question, but Cheshire was somewhere else all of a sudden. The noise he heard, with its rising and falling action, was familiar and he sorted through his memories in an attempt to place it.

Anselm asked another question. Cheshire paid no attention. There was something to the noise, something so close, like a word on the tip of his tongue. Cheshire stood up and slapped the dust from his clothes. Anselm was looking at him but stopped asking questions. He knew his master well, and knew when to shut up and let Cheshire think.

The panic hit Cheshire seconds before the revelation, as if his heart solved the puzzle before his mind did. A rising terror in tune with the gravely sound filled Cheshire with such fear he almost lost the answer before it came to his mouth.

“Breathing,” Cheshire whispered, not because he was worried about making noise, but because a whisper was all he had strength for.

Then there was a new noise, something crumbling and cracking. Cheshire’s mind, slowed by fear, didn’t register it, and didn’t connect it with the illusion of Anselm becoming taller.

The floor fell out beneath Cheshire. He had a sudden sensation of levitation before freefall stole his breath from him. He managed to grab onto the flooring near Anselm’s feet long enough for Anselm to reach down and grab his other hand. Rock and rubble fell around him, down into the murky depths.

“Master!” Anselm shouted, balancing himself. “I’ve got you. Hold on!”

Cheshire didn’t even look at him. Below the rhythmic sound of breathing ceased. He looked down into the darkness. At the same time something looked back up at him.

A massive eye as wide in diameter as Cheshire was tall, glowed a blazing orange at him. At first the pupil was a cloud of squid’s ink against water. Then in an instant it focused. The black in the eye became a long slit into an even greater darkness than what surrounded the eye. That slit of a pupil, black as the darkness between stars, stared at Cheshire for the space of two or three heartbeats. But to Cheshire, he had been seen by a power beyond his wildest imaginings and saw himself as a flea before the gaze of some ancient god.

Panic like lightning bolts rippled down his legs and up his arms, stealing his strength. His fingers loosened their grip on Anselm and the floor without his control. He willed them to close again, to hold on. It was impossible. He felt like he was trying to make another person’s body do something just by thinking it.

“Master,” Anselm cried, “you’re slipping. I need your help. Master!”

Cheshire looked down once more. The pupil of the great eye beheld him for another second, and then went cloudy again. The perfectly circular eye narrowed, lingered that way for a moment, and then closed.

Cheshire felt strength return to his arms and legs. He turned away from the eye in the darkness, knowing it was still there, but not wanting to think about it. He grabbed Anselm’s arm with firm strength this time. The boy groaned and growled but Cheshire felt himself rising. Anselm grabbed at something out of view, and continued to pull Cheshire.

“That’s it,” Anselm said. “I’ve got you.”

Cheshire grinned as he strained to pull himself up. He looked at Anselm soon enough to see the boy’s eyes fixate on the depths below. The boy’s face went white as wool and his mouth hung open. His pupils grew to nearly encapsulate the color of his eyes.

“Master.” It was barely audible.

Cheshire didn’t look but he could *feel* the eye far below him opening once more. “No, Anselm! No! Look at me.”

“Master,” Anselm said again. Cheshire felt the boy’s hold on him growing slack.

“Anselm! Anse-”

The boy’s grip relaxed. Cheshire lost hold of him. His upper body swung backwards. He lunged his arms to the boy. Anselm tried to reach but he was too far.

Cheshire fell. Unable to shout, all he heard was the breathing of that immense beast below and the sound of Anselm screaming his name before the darkness took him.

## Chapter Four

*The Kingdom of Hearts*

King Declan III, the sovereign of the Kingdom of Hearts, strolled through the corridors of his royal palace, cradling his infant son, Leland. The child babbled as they walked, and Declan only broke his tender engagement with him to acknowledge the bows of servants and guardsmen with a warm smile. The Kingdom of Hearts, renowned as the oldest, strongest, and wealthiest of the living kingdoms, was embodied by the grandeur of its royal palace.

They passed through a doorway guarded by eight-foot tall dragons with golden scales and ruby eyes, traversing a hallway of the purest white marble. Wide, arched windows revealed the lush gardens below on one side, while flawless silver mirrors on the other bathed the corridor in light. As they turned down a corridor lined with golden suits of armor, their helms carved with roaring lion faces and shields emblazoned with red hearts, Declan was reminded of the palace's significance as a beacon of society and culture.

“You know, Leland,” Declan said, kissing the side of his son's forehead, “it was our ancestor who vanquished the darkness and restored light to the world. His descendants built this palace and Corvix Bero, our magnificent capital city, around it.” Leland's response was unintelligible, but Declan continued to engage him, tickling his nose and making playful faces.

“Corvix Bero, Leland,” Declan nodded, “the greatest city in all the realms. The Kingdom of Hearts enabled humanity to emerge from its caves and bask in the sunlight once more.”

As they proceeded, Declan paused by voluminous red curtains obscuring a series of grand arched windows. Drawing them back, he gazed upon the courtyard teeming with life—people talking, laughing, working, trading. “Look at the prosperity we've brought to this kingdom and beyond. It's only fitting that all in the realms, whose lives we've enriched, should bow before the King of Hearts.” Letting the curtains fall, he continued his walk.

Interlacing tiles of black and white shone beneath their feet, so polished that Declan could have used them as a mirror. “And someday, you will inherit this mantle,” he said, nuzzling Leland's face, eliciting a giggle and an affectionate grasp at his nose.

Upon entering the nursery, Declan was greeted by a floor scattered with toys and a large rectangular table in the center, a fixture since his grandfather's childhood. Atop it was a detailed sculpted map of the known realms, oriented to true north. The Kingdom of Hearts was prominently displayed, bordered by the Black Mountains and the Dawn Sea. Surrounding realms were meticulously crafted, with major cities, towns, and routes etched into the base and painted in vivid detail. Small figurines representing armies and navies were strewn across the map.

Alistair, Declan's eldest son, was near the table, absorbed in the painted figurines. “Ah, Alistair,” Declan greeted, “strategizing our next moves, I see.” Alistair, having earlier maneuvered a division of the Red Legion through Suicide Gap, the sole pass large enough for an army to traverse to the Kingdom of Spades, simply shrugged.

Declan shook his head. “Your forces would be decimated there.” He demonstrated an alternative strategy, moving the army north and coordinating a naval rendezvous to circumvent the enemy's defenses. “That's the art of war, son. We're already assembling a fleet in these fjords to flank their defenses.” Alistair nodded, then repositioned a few figurines to Suicide Gap.

“A holding action,” Declan observed, to which Alistair nodded again, meeting his father's gaze.

Turning to Leland, Declan said, “If you surpass my legacy as king one day, I'll harbor no resentment.” Alistair's interjection, “That's saying a lot,” was followed by the nursemaid's approach. With her deep brown skin and ebony hair, she was a striking figure, her attire befitting her role as caretaker to the future king—a cream dress with a blue bustier and petticoats that accentuated her figure.

“Thank you for returning my little prince,” she said, reaching for Leland. Declan handed over the infant with a reluctant smile, addressing her as Deanne.

“It's Di-,” she began to correct, but Declan interjected, “Of course, Diana.”

“It's Dinah, Your Majesty,” she corrected, her focus on the children.

Declan lifted her chin, locking eyes with her. “How could I forget?” he said, his smile lingering as Dinah blushed and turned away.
